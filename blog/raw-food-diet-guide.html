Raw Food Diet for Dogs: Benefits, Risks, and How to Start

The raw food movement for dogs has grown from a niche practice among breeders and working-dog owners into a mainstream feeding philosophy. Proponents claim it delivers shinier coats, cleaner teeth, higher energy, and better overall health. Critics point to bacterial risks, nutritional imbalances, and a lack of long-term scientific studies.

So where does the truth lie? This guide examines what raw feeding actually involves, the evidence for and against it, and how to do it safely if you decide it's right for your dog.

What Is the BARF Diet?

BARF stands for Biologically Appropriate Raw Food (some expand it as Bones And Raw Food). The concept was popularized by Australian veterinarian Dr. Ian Billinghurst in the 1990s. The philosophy is straightforward: dogs evolved eating raw meat, bones, and plant matter, so their digestive systems are optimized for these foods rather than highly processed kibble.

A standard BARF diet follows these approximate ratios:

  • 70% muscle meat (chicken, beef, turkey, lamb, fish)
  • 10% raw edible bone (chicken necks, wings, duck frames)
  • 10% organ meat (5% liver, 5% other organs like kidney, spleen, or brain)
  • 10% vegetables and fruits (leafy greens, broccoli, carrots, blueberries)

Some raw feeders also follow the "Prey Model" approach, which eliminates plant matter entirely and aims to replicate the proportions of a whole prey animal: 80% meat, 10% bone, and 10% organs.

Potential Benefits of Raw Feeding

Dog owners who switch to raw diets commonly report the following improvements, though scientific evidence supporting some claims remains limited:

Observed Benefits

  • Improved coat quality: Many owners notice shinier, softer fur within weeks of transitioning. The higher fat content in raw diets may contribute to this.
  • Dental health: Chewing raw meaty bones can reduce plaque and tartar buildup. A 2016 study in the Journal of Veterinary Dentistry found raw bone chewing significantly reduced dental calculus.
  • Smaller, firmer stools: Raw-fed dogs typically produce less waste because the food is more digestible with less filler.
  • Reduced allergies: Some dogs with food sensitivities improve on raw diets, possibly because they're avoiding common allergens found in processed foods like wheat, corn, or soy.
  • Improved energy and body composition: Higher protein and fat with lower carbohydrates can support lean muscle mass.

Risks and Concerns

Raw feeding is not without significant considerations. Being aware of these risks allows you to manage them effectively.

Bacterial Contamination

Raw meat can carry Salmonella, E. coli, Listeria, and other pathogens. While dogs are more resistant to these bacteria than humans, they are not immune. A 2012 study found that 7% of raw-fed dogs shed Salmonella in their feces compared to 0% of kibble-fed dogs. This poses risks not just to the dog but to household members, especially young children, elderly people, and immunocompromised individuals.

Nutritional Imbalance

Formulating a complete and balanced raw diet is more complex than scooping kibble. Common deficiencies in improperly formulated raw diets include:

  • Calcium and phosphorus ratio imbalances (especially if bone content is wrong)
  • Zinc and copper deficiencies
  • Vitamin D and vitamin E shortages
  • Omega-3 fatty acid insufficiency

Bone Hazards

While raw bones are softer than cooked bones and less likely to splinter, they can still cause tooth fractures, esophageal obstructions, or intestinal blockages, particularly weight-bearing bones from large animals.

Cost and Convenience

Raw feeding typically costs 2-5 times more than premium kibble and requires dedicated freezer space, careful meal prep, and strict hygiene protocols. Use our Dog Food Calculator to estimate how much raw food your dog needs daily.

Food Safety Best Practices

If you choose raw feeding, these hygiene practices are non-negotiable:

  • Keep raw meat frozen until 24-48 hours before serving; thaw in the refrigerator
  • Use dedicated cutting boards and utensils for raw pet food preparation
  • Wash hands thoroughly with soap and hot water after handling raw food
  • Clean food bowls with hot soapy water after every meal
  • Disinfect all prep surfaces with a pet-safe sanitizer
  • Do not leave raw food at room temperature for more than 20 minutes
  • Pick up any uneaten food promptly
  • Store raw food separately from human food in the freezer and refrigerator

How to Transition to a Raw Diet

Switching abruptly from kibble to raw food can cause digestive upset. A gradual transition gives your dog's gut microbiome time to adjust.

The 14-Day Transition Plan

Days Kibble Raw Food Notes
1-3 75% 25% Start with one protein source (chicken is easiest)
4-7 50% 50% Monitor stool consistency
8-10 25% 75% Introduce a second protein if stools are firm
11-14 0% 100% Full raw diet with proper ratios

Some practitioners recommend feeding kibble and raw at separate meals rather than mixing them in the same bowl, since they digest at different rates. However, many dogs handle the combination without issues.

Which Dogs Benefit Most from Raw Diets?

While any healthy adult dog can potentially thrive on a properly formulated raw diet, certain dogs may benefit more than others:

  • Dogs with food sensitivities or allergies: Raw diets allow complete control over ingredients, making elimination diets easier
  • Working and sporting dogs: The high protein and fat content supports intense physical demands
  • Dogs with chronic skin issues: Removing processed ingredients and adding healthy fats can improve skin health
  • Dogs with dental problems: Raw meaty bones provide natural teeth cleaning

Dogs That Should Avoid Raw Diets

  • Puppies under 12 weeks (precise nutrition during rapid growth is critical)
  • Immunocompromised dogs or those on immunosuppressive drugs
  • Dogs in households with immunocompromised people, infants, or elderly residents
  • Dogs with pancreatitis or other conditions requiring low-fat diets

Commercial Raw vs. DIY Raw

If the idea of formulating meals yourself feels overwhelming, commercial raw brands offer pre-made options that are nutritionally balanced and tested for pathogens. These come in frozen patties, freeze-dried nuggets, or fresh rolls. They cost more than DIY raw but remove much of the complexity and food safety risk.

If you prefer DIY, strongly consider consulting a board-certified veterinary nutritionist to formulate recipes specific to your dog's needs. Online recipe generators vary widely in quality and many produce nutritionally incomplete diets.

The Bottom Line

Raw feeding is neither a miracle cure nor a dangerous fad. When done properly with attention to nutritional balance, food safety, and veterinary guidance, it can be a valid feeding approach for many dogs. The key word is "properly." A poorly formulated raw diet is far worse than a quality commercial kibble.
